Types of Traditional Attires

Bilas

The bilas is a traditional skirt made from plant fibers, worn by women in many PNG societies. It's often decorated with shells, beads, and feathers, symbolizing wealth and status. Traditional “bilas” in Papua New Guinea refers to elaborate body decoration and dress used for singsings (cultural festivals), where each tribe showcases its identity. A fun fact is that bilas often includes real bird-of-paradise feathers, shells, and natural dyes, and no two groups style it the same—so you can tell where someone is from just by their bilas.

Koteka

The koteka, or penis gourd, is a traditional male garment worn by some tribes in Papua New Guinea. It's a hollowed-out gourd that serves as a cover, often decorated with intricate carvings.

The koteka is a traditional garment worn by men in the highlands of Papua New Guinea, made from a dried gourd and used to cover the genitals instead of modern clothing. Its shape and size vary between tribes, often reflecting identity, status, or cultural meaning, with some designed to point upward or outward. It is secured with strings around the body and is commonly worn in daily life as well as during ceremonies and festivals. Despite outside influences, including efforts like Operasi Koteka, the koteka remains an important symbol of cultural heritage and tradition among highland communities.

headdresses

Traditional headdresses in Papua New Guinea are colorful and special items made from feathers, shells, and natural materials. Different tribes have different designs, and they can show a person’s identity or status. People wear them mostly during festivals and ceremonies, where they help express culture, pride, and tradition.

Face Painting

Traditional face painting uses natural pigments to create intricate designs that signify tribal identity, status, or ceremonial purposes. Patterns often include geometric shapes and symbolic motifs.In Papua New Guinea, traditional face painting is a vivid cultural symbol. Made from natural pigments like clay and ochre, designs show tribe, status, or spiritual intent. Often paired with feathers and costumes, some patterns intimidate or invoke spirits, as with the famous Asaro Mudmen. Today, it thrives in festivals and ceremonies, keeping ancient traditions alive.

necklaces

Ornate necklaces made from shells, bones, teeth, and beads are worn to display wealth and social standing. Some necklaces are used in bride price ceremonies. Traditional necklaces in Papua New Guinea are made from natural materials like shells, bones, beads, and feathers, serving as symbols of social status, tribal identity, and spiritual beliefs. Their designs vary by region and often convey cultural stories, ancestral connections, and community values, making them both decorative and meaningful.

Armbands

Armbands crafted from shells, bones, or woven fibers adorn the arms and signify achievements or clan affiliations.In Papua New Guinea, traditional armbands are crafted from natural materials such as fibers, shells, feathers, and animal skins. They are worn during ceremonies, dances, and rituals to display social status, tribal identity, and achievements. The designs vary between tribes, often reflecting spiritual beliefs, ancestral connections, and cultural heritage, making them both decorative and symbolic expressions of community identity.

Nose Piercings

Nose piercings with bone, shell, or feather ornaments are common among many tribes, often indicating marital status or social rankIn Papua New Guinea, traditional nose piercings were important cultural symbols that marked adulthood and initiation, showed a person’s status or bravery, and often carried spiritual meaning such as protection or connection to ancestors. People used materials like bone, shell, or pig tusks, and styles varied widely between different tribes, especially in the Sepik region..

Ear Ornaments

Large ear plugs or ornaments made from shells, bones, or wood stretch the earlobes and are worn as symbols of beauty and status.In Papua New Guinea, some traditional ear ornaments are so large that they stretch the earlobes dramatically, sometimes nearly to the shoulders. Made from natural materials like shells and feathers, they symbolize beauty, identity, and social status in many Highland cultures.

Wigs

Elaborate wigs made from human hair or fibers are worn during ceremonies, often decorated with feathers and shells.In Papua New Guinea, Huli men, called “Wigmen,” wear elaborate wigs made from their hair and decorated with feathers and fur. These wigs symbolize adulthood, status, and tribal identity and are worn during ceremonies, festivals, and important cultural events.

Feathered Capes

Capes adorned with colorful bird feathers are worn by men during dances and rituals, representing power and connection to nature. Papua New Guinea’s traditional feather capes are ceremonial garments made from bird feathers and plant fibers, worn by men of high status. Their vibrant designs symbolize rank, clan, and spiritual connections, enhancing movement in dances and rituals. They are celebrated cultural artifacts displayed in museums worldwide.

Shell Money

Shell money in Papua New Guinea is a traditional form of wealth made from carefully crafted shells, used mainly for ceremonies, bride price, and settling disputes rather than everyday shopping. Different regions like the Tolai people and Bougainville have unique styles, and its value depends on length and quality. Its still important today, symbolizing status, relationships, and cultural identity, often passed down through generations.

Bark Cloth

Cloth made from beaten bark, painted with natural dyes, used for clothing and ceremonial purposes in some regions.Traditional bark cloth is made by beating the inner bark of trees into thin sheets. Used for clothing, mats, and ceremonies, it is often decorated with natural dyes and patterns, reflecting rich cultural traditions.

Feathered Skirts

Skirts made from layers of feathers, worn during dances, create a dramatic visual effect and represent tribal identity.In Papua New Guinea, traditional feather skirts, often called bilas skirts, are vibrant garments made from bird feathers, fibers, and plant materials. Worn during dances, ceremonies, and festivals, they showcase intricate patterns and bright colors, reflecting a wearer’s tribe, status, and cultural heritage. These skirts are both artistic expressions and important symbols of identity.

Feather Decorations

Various feather arrangements attached to clothing or worn separately, symbolizing connection to birds and spiritual realms.PNG traditional feather decorations are vibrant feathered ornaments, often from birds of paradise or cassowaries, used in headdresses, masks, and ceremonial attire. They symbolize status, clan identity, and spiritual beliefs, and are central to dances, rituals, and cultural festivals across Papua New Guinea.

Bone Ornaments

Ornaments made from animal bones, worn as pendants or earrings, representing hunting prowess and ancestral connections. Papua New Guinea traditional bone ornaments are handmade from animal (and sometimes human) bones and are worn as symbols of status, wealth, and spiritual power. Theyre especially used in ceremonies, with items like nose bones showing maturity or warrior identity. Often combined with feathers and shells, these ornaments vary by tribe and are still worn today at events like the Mount Hagen Cultural Show.

Shell decorations

Decorative items made from shells, used in various forms of adornment, symbolizing wealth from coastal resources. Papua New Guinea’s traditional shell decorations symbolize wealth, identity, and cultural pride, with shells once used as currency and still featured in important ceremonies and exchange systems like the Kula ring, as well as in colorful sing-sings celebrating heritage and community.

Pig Tusks

Pig tusks worn as necklaces or attached to belts, signifying wealth and status in pig-raising cultures. In Papua New Guinea, traditional pig tusks are valued symbols of wealth and status, especially in highland communities. They are used in ceremonies like bride price and represent cultural pride, as raising pigs with large, curved tusks takes time and care.

Grass Armbands
